# Section a — State-of-the-Art and Objectives

## A. Clinical problem and unmet need

Barrett's oesophagus (BE) is the only known precursor to oesophageal adenocarcinoma (EAC), a cancer whose incidence has risen markedly in Western populations over recent decades and whose prognosis, once clinically manifest, remains poor (Curtius et al., 2021; Shaheen et al., 2022; Vaughan & Fitzgerald, 2015). The clinical challenge BE poses is one of precision risk stratification: most patients carry a lifetime progression risk of less than one percent per year and will never develop cancer, yet a minority harbour disease trajectories that lead to EAC within years, and current surveillance practice cannot reliably distinguish them at the level of the individual patient (Bao et al., 2023; Paulson et al., 2022). Endoscopic sampling and histological dysplasia grading — the twin pillars of BE management — are limited for individual-level risk decisions: histological assessment is subject to substantial interobserver variability, particularly for the low-grade and indefinite categories that are the most common and clinically consequential, and even the most meticulous surveillance protocols sample only a small fraction of the Barrett's segment, making consequential sampling error a structural limitation of the current approach rather than a correctable technical one (Choi et al., 2022; Montgomery et al., 2001; Vennalaganti et al., 2017). The result is a surveillance system that simultaneously overtreats the majority who will never progress and under-detects the minority who will — an unresolved clinical tension that requires validated computational biomarkers integrating tissue architecture and immune ecology to address. I hypothesize that computational pathology measures of tissue architecture and immune ecology will predict the risk of progression to oesophageal adenocarcinoma.


## B. State of the art — biological understanding

Dysplasia grading has structured BE surveillance for decades, and it retains clinical value at the group level: the four-category scheme — none, indefinite, low-grade, high-grade — correlates with molecular changes and with the probability of EAC at the population scale (Killcoyne & Fitzgerald, 2021). But as a biomarker for the individual patient it is insufficiently discriminating on several counts. Dysplasia is frequently focal and may not be endoscopically visible; most surveillance protocols sample a small minority of the BE segment, making sampling error an inherent structural problem rather than a correctable technical one (Choi et al., 2022; Codipilly et al., 2022). The histological diagnosis itself is contested: interobserver variability is substantial, particularly for low-grade and indefinite categories (Montgomery et al., 2001; Vennalaganti et al., 2017). These limitations do not reflect inadequate pathological skill; they reflect the limits of what histomorphological grading, applied to focal biopsies from a spatially heterogeneous segment, can resolve without additional biological information. Capturing the spatial heterogeneity of dysplastic change — at the resolution of individual crypts, across the biopsy, and along the full Barrett's segment — requires a computational framework that operates at the scale of the tissue unit.

Earlier in the morphological progression sequence, crypt architectural atypia — subtle abnormalities of glandular organisation that precede frank dysplasia — has been advocated as a morphological predictor of progression risk detectable before the dysplasia threshold is crossed {CITE: Odze crypt atypia papers}. Robert Odze, a planned collaborator on this project and a recognised authority on BE pathology, has characterised the architectural and cellular features that define atypical crypts and their relationship to malignant potential; clinical implementation has been limited by the same interobserver variability that undermines frank dysplasia grading. Computationally designed tools that encode crypt-level architecture from H&E images offer a principled and reproducible route to operationalising these morphological criteria at clinical scale.

The genomic basis of BE progression has been extensively characterised. TP53 mutation, copy number instability, and clonal expansion provide a mechanistic framework for the neoplastic sequence (CITE: Fitzgerald group genomics papers on TP53 and copy number instability in BE), and multi-region genomic studies have established that distinct evolutionary lineages segregate spatially within the same BE segment — TP53 mutations and somatic copy number variants occupying distinct clonal populations, with TP53-negative clone expansion systematically missed by single-biopsy designs (Martinez et al., 2018; Paulson et al., 2022). These findings confirm that spatial heterogeneity is the norm in BE and that any biomarker strategy that ignores the spatial architecture of the segment is structurally incomplete. Yet genomics answers only part of the biology: clonal identity does not determine cell state, and the translation of a genomic event into a tissue-level phenotype depends on context that DNA sequencing cannot resolve. A computational spatial framework that encodes the tissue architecture within which these genomic events are expressed — resolving the positional relationships between distinct cell and crypt populations across the segment — provides the necessary complement to molecular profiling.

Recent work has demonstrated that transcriptional reprogramming — not clonal replacement — is the dominant tissue-level response to selective pressure during EAC progression (Barroux et al., 2025), and that the same genomic event produces opposite effects on immune infiltration and cell cycle regulation depending on disease stage and tissue context (Ganguli et al., 2025; Ercan et al., 2025). Cancer progression in BE is determined by the tissue ecosystem — the aggregate of epithelial cell states, immune composition, stromal architecture, and their spatial relationships — not by genomic alterations in isolation. Transcriptional analysis begins to access this level, but the methods that have produced these insights cannot resolve where within the tissue these programs are active, which cell types they involve simultaneously, or how they are organised spatially relative to the morphological features that pathologists use to assess disease. Bridging transcriptional and morphological resolution requires a computational system that characterises cell and crypt identity at the spatial scale of the tissue.

The immune microenvironment of BE undergoes a characteristic sequence of changes as the disease progresses toward EAC: increased recruitment of inflammatory immune cells, mixed pro- and anti-inflammatory cytokine secretion, and a stepwise increase in expression of immune checkpoint molecules — including PD-L1 — from non-dysplastic BE through dysplasia to carcinoma (Campos et al., 2020; Lagisetty et al., 2021; Porter et al., 2020; Wagener-Ryczek et al., 2020). BE arises in chronically inflamed mucosa damaged by acid reflux, and its progression unfolds within this inflammatory context. While acute inflammation has tumour-suppressive effects, chronic unresolved inflammation is an enabling hallmark of cancer, promoting clonal selection and supporting the immunological remodelling that accompanies malignant transformation (Hanahan, 2022; Bhattacharya et al., 2025). These group-level characterisations establish that immune change accompanies progression — but they do not establish whether the spatial organisation of immune cells, which niches form relative to the epithelium, and how they evolve within individual patients over time, carries information about future risk that is independent of the composition-level signal. Resolving this requires an immune ecology model that operates at the spatial scale of individual tissue niches and that is anchored within the epithelial architecture of the biopsy.

The evolutionary dynamics of BE extend beyond the region of visible dysplasia. Field cancerisation — the extension of molecular and microenvironmental changes into adjacent histologically normal-appearing mucosa — raises the possibility that the spatial immune and stromal landscape of non-dysplastic tissue reflects regional disease risk before morphological atypia becomes detectable (Zeki et al., 2011). The stromal changes caused by conditions that produce BE, and then amplified by BE progression itself, alter immune functionality and shape the microenvironment in ways that may promote clonal growth in adjacent non-dysplastic epithelium; studying non-dysplastic regions therefore holds substantial potential for early risk detection. Colorectal carcinogenesis provides a well-characterised model for neoplastic progression in the gastrointestinal epithelium, and spatially-resolved multi-omic profiling in colorectal cancer has shown that immune exclusion signatures linked to chromosomal instability are accessible only through multi-region spatial analysis, invisible to single-region sampling (Heiser et al., 2023) — and chromosomal instability is a defining feature of BE progression. Within this ecosystem of change, even the spatial positioning of individual cellular events within tissue architecture encodes biological signal: the location of mitotic figures within Barrett's crypts — at the base versus the luminal surface — carries prognostic significance beyond a simple mitotic count (CITE: spatial significance of mitotic figure location in BE or GI epithelial progression), pointing to the importance of positional context within the tissue hierarchy for disease-relevant information. Whether these spatial signals are accessible through computational analysis of the tissue architecture — at the scale of the crypt, the immune niche, and the whole segment — is a question this project is positioned to answer.

Taken together, these lines of evidence converge on a common conclusion: no single level of analysis — histological, genomic, transcriptomic, or immunological — is sufficient alone. The spatial organisation of the tissue ecosystem, measured simultaneously at cell, crypt, immune niche, and whole-segment scales and across time in individual patients, is the level at which the determinants of progression are encoded — and it is this level that current approaches do not reach.


## C. State of the art — computational approaches

Computational pathology methods have demonstrated robust performance across histopathological tasks, and their application to Barrett's oesophagus is accelerating. Automated analysis pipelines applied to Cytosponge specimens have achieved diagnostic accuracy at population scale without per-slide pathologist input (Bouzid et al., 2024), and computational pathology models have begun to address BE grading and progression risk in ways that extend the reach of routine pathology (CITE: computational pathology models for BE progression risk stratification). {NOTE: PI to rephrase} The approaches that have scaled most successfully, however, treat tissue as a collection of image patches and aggregate features by global pooling — a design that achieves scale by discarding the biological identity of tissue units and the spatial relationships between them. Multiple instance learning systems using foundation model features represent the current frontier, but they are optimised for slide-level prediction rather than spatially-resolved characterisation of tissue organisation and its evolution over time. What is needed instead is a system that represents tissue at the level of its biological units — cells and crypts — and shares a single learned encoder across a panel of downstream prediction tasks.

Graph-based representations address this limitation directly. By encoding tissue elements — cells, crypts, stromal regions — as nodes and their spatial relationships as edges, graph neural networks (GNNs) capture both local histological detail and global tissue organisation in a single representational framework (CITE: GNN-based approaches in computational pathology). This structure aligns with the biological hierarchy of BE tissue: individual columnar cells within crypts, crypts within tissue regions, and tissue regions within their immune and stromal context. The method applies to computational pathology images regardless of staining technique, making it scalable to the large cohorts that precancer research requires. Because interactions between distinct cell types within the tissue microenvironment are inherently heterogeneous — goblet cells adjacent to foveolar cells within the same crypt, immune niches adjacent to epithelial compartments — heterophilic GNN designs, which decouple node feature updating from neighbourhood aggregation and thereby preserve the distinct representations of biologically different cell populations, are the appropriate architecture for this setting. Self-supervised pre-training on large unlabelled cohorts enables transferable tissue representations to be learned without abundant labelled progression outcomes — a practical necessity in precancer research, where progression events are rare (Zhang et al., 2022). Hierarchical graph decomposition — encoding individual cells within crypt subgraphs, and crypt representations within tissue-level graphs — reduces the computational cost of self-supervised pre-training on large whole-slide images to a tractable scale while preserving the biological resolution that flat graph encoders discard. A recent study demonstrated the effectiveness of self-supervised GNNs applied to histopathology images (Öğüt et al., 2026), establishing that spatially-aware graph representations can be learned from unlabelled tissue without manual annotation; yet no current system applies multi-scale hierarchical graph pre-training to BE tissue at cell and crypt resolution, nor demonstrates that a single pre-trained encoder supports a panel of clinically distinct downstream prediction tasks. Demonstrating such a system in Barrett's oesophagus — where cell-type heterogeneity, glandular organisation, and immune context are simultaneously informative — is the computational objective of this project.

Spatial profiling has begun to characterise the BE and EAC microenvironment at the protein level. Lin et al. (2025) identified progression-predictive immune neighbourhoods in BE using 56-plex spatial proteomics; Strasser et al. (2025) mapped concerted epithelial–stromal remodelling across the BE→EAC progression spectrum using CODEX multiplexed imaging. These studies establish that the spatial organisation of immune and stromal compartments carries biological and prognostic information, but they are launching pads, not solutions: protein panels are constrained to a pre-selected limited marker panel and cannot resolve the transcriptional programs and regulatory logic that underlie the populations they detect. Graph-based integration of multi-compartment spatial data has been implemented in oncology contexts — ATHENA (Martinelli & Rapsomaniki, 2022) provides a computational framework for relating tumour heterogeneity, graph topology, and spatial omics features — but no analogous integrative framework has been applied to the precancer system in which both disease-preceding and disease-state tissue are co-present within the same specimen. Spatially-resolved transcriptomics applied along the precancer–cancer axis in adjacent carcinogenesis systems has revealed what whole-transcriptome spatial resolution makes accessible: Chang et al. (2025) mapped a cancer-associated fibroblast–epithelial niche at the precancer–cancer boundary in oesophageal squamous cell carcinogenesis; Kader et al. (2024) traced the progressive shift from immune surveillance to immune suppression along the fallopian tube precancer axis; Zhu et al. (2025) identified TIM-3 as a precancer-specific immune evasion target in lung adenocarcinoma precursors — a finding inaccessible to cancer-stage profiling alone. Spatially-resolved whole-transcriptome profiling has not been applied to paired BE precursor and EAC tissue from the same patient at single-cell resolution; no study has captured the transcriptional transition within individual patients in whom both disease states are co-present in the same resection specimen. No computational or molecular framework currently integrates epithelial architecture, immune ecology, and transcriptional resolution in a single analytical system applied to Barrett's oesophagus. This integration — epithelial architecture, immune ecology, and transcriptional resolution from the same tissue — is what the proposed project is designed to deliver.


## D. The gap — outstanding questions

The converging limitations of each approach taken alone — the spatial imprecision of histological grading, the structural incompleteness of single-biopsy genomics, the inability of protein panels to resolve transcriptional programs, and the absence of multi-scale hierarchical computational models for BE tissue — define a cluster of unanswered questions at the intersection of biology, computation, and clinical translation. Answering them requires a framework that operates simultaneously at the scale of individual cells, tissue architecture, immune ecology, and whole-transcriptome spatial programs, and that applies these measures longitudinally within the same patients. This project is motivated by the following outstanding questions:

1. Does the spatial organisation of immune cells in non-dysplastic Barrett's mucosa carry information about future progression risk that is invisible to current histological grading?

2. Does the immune microenvironment of non-dysplastic Barrett's epithelium reflect the regional disease state of the whole segment when dysplasia is present elsewhere in the same patient?

3. Does the spatial distribution of morphologically distinct epithelial cell subtypes within and across Barrett's crypts — beyond the goblet/non-goblet dichotomy — provide both independent information about dysplasia grade and progression risk, and a more reproducible and discriminating assessment than current histological grading?

4. Does the immune microenvironment of Barrett's mucosa follow a directional trajectory from immune surveillance toward immune tolerance within individual patients as the epithelium transitions from non-dysplastic metaplasia to dysplasia?

5. Can self-supervised representations learned from the spatial graph of cells and crypts in H&E whole-slide images encode histological features of Barrett's tissue architecture that generalise across downstream prediction tasks without label-intensive training?

6. What transcriptional programs distinguish Barrett's epithelial, immune, and stromal cells across the transition from non-dysplastic metaplasia to adenocarcinoma within individual patients, and do these programs co-localise with the morphological and ecological features identified by computational analysis?

7. Do cancer-associated fibroblast subtypes and stromal–immune interactions at the Barrett's–EAC interface form spatially-structured immunosuppressive niches analogous to those identified in oesophageal squamous and other epithelial carcinogenesis systems?


## E. The proposal

This project addresses these questions through three sequential and integrated layers of analysis — an epithelial graph model, an immune ecology model, and a spatial transcriptomic discovery programme — each consuming the outputs of the layer before it and each grounded in complementary cohorts: a large longitudinal natural history cohort of Barrett's patients that provides the endoscopic biopsy archive for Aims 1 and 2, and a CHUV in-house cohort of EAC resection specimens that provides the paired Barrett's–EAC tissue for Aim 3.

**Aim 1** builds a multi-scale hierarchical graph neural network over H&E whole-slide images of BE, encoding tissue architecture simultaneously at the level of individual cells, crypts, and tissue regions through a self-supervised pre-training objective that requires no progression labels during learning. Applied to a panel of downstream tasks — dysplasia classification, progression prediction, columnar cell characterisation, and BE subtyping — it directly addresses whether spatial cell-type organisation beyond the goblet/non-goblet dichotomy carries independent and more discriminating prognostic information than current histological assessment (Q3), and whether a single pre-trained encoder generalises across clinically distinct downstream tasks without label-intensive retraining (Q5). The output — a graph autoencoder encoding the structural invariants of BE tissue — is passed as a frozen encoder to Aim 2, making every downstream immune analysis morphology-aware from the outset. Without a validated, spatially-resolved computational measure of epithelial state at cell and crypt resolution, neither the progression-associated immune dynamics of Aim 2 nor the transcriptional programs of Aim 3 can be anchored to the morphological substrate that gives them biological meaning.

**Aim 2** builds a hierarchical immune cell graph directly within the tissue architecture established by the frozen Aim 1 encoder, capturing immune cell organisation at three levels — individual cells, local Voronoi niches, and tissue regions — and applying it across analyses that exploit the longitudinal structure of the cohort. It tests whether the spatial organisation of immune cells in non-dysplastic mucosa carries pre-dysplastic progression information (Q1), whether the immune landscape of non-dysplastic tissue reflects the regional field-wide disease state when dysplasia is present elsewhere in the segment (Q2), and whether immune remodelling follows a directed trajectory from surveillance toward tolerance within individual patients over years of documented follow-up (Q4). The integration of ecological spatial statistics — Shannon diversity index, Ripley's K/L-function, mean nearest-neighbour distances — as explicit node-level features within the graph hierarchy bridges landscape ecology methodology and tissue biology in a single representational system, providing an interpretable layer between the deep representations and the biological phenomena they encode. Without a temporally-resolved immune ecology model operating at the scale of individual tissue niches, the pre-dysplastic window of risk — the interval in which immune dysregulation precedes visible morphological change and in which surveillance adjustment has the greatest potential to alter disease trajectory — remains inaccessible.

**Aim 3** applies Xenium spatial transcriptomics to multi-region EAC resection specimens containing paired BE and EAC tissue from the same patients, providing the transcriptional resolution that neither the morphological graph models nor protein-level spatial panels can supply. Guided by Aim 1 crypt atypia maps and Aim 2 immune niche profiles for region-of-interest selection, it characterises the transcriptional programs that co-localise with morphological and ecological progression features within individual patients (Q6), and tests whether cancer-associated fibroblast subtypes form spatially-structured immunosuppressive niches at the BE–EAC interface analogous to those described in adjacent carcinogenesis systems (Q7). The intra-patient paired design — multiple regions from the same resection specimen at single-cell spatial resolution — is structurally unavailable from endoscopic biopsy cohorts and eliminates inter-patient confounding from the analysis of the BE→EAC transcriptional transition. Without the transcriptional ground truth this aim provides, the biological programs that the upstream morphological and immune models approximate but cannot directly access — the regulatory logic of immune niches, the stromal programs that structure the tumour-permissive microenvironment — would remain unresolved, limiting the mechanistic interpretability and translational relevance of the computational biomarkers from Aims 1 and 2.


## F. Impact and new horizons

If this project succeeds, it delivers the first validated computational system for BE that resolves epithelial morphology, immune ecology, and their co-evolution simultaneously from routine H&E slides — without additional assay cost — and produces quantitative biomarkers of individual-level progression risk that current pathological assessment cannot provide: for Barrett's surveillance specifically, a validated multi-scale graph classifier could stratify patients at their index endoscopy, identifying the minority at genuine risk for intensified monitoring while sparing the majority unnecessary intervention, with the pre-dysplastic immune signal analysis extending this to the earliest ecologically detectable window before any morphological change is visible. For precancer biology more broadly, the moment to study the co-evolution of epithelium and immune microenvironment in situ — within the natural history of the lesion, before excision closes the window — is precisely the interval that BE provides: the longitudinal within-patient biopsy archive generated by BE's non-excision management paradigm is structurally unavailable in the colon, lung, or stomach, making BE a model precancer system for questions about immune and epithelial co-evolution under sustained selective pressure, and the methods developed here are directly transferable to any precancer characterised by chronic mucosal inflammation and a measurable natural history. For the computational pathology field, the graph autoencoder trained on BE tissue establishes the architecture and pre-training framework for biologically-stratified multi-scale graph representations in histopathology — a design principle applicable wherever cell-type heterogeneity, glandular organisation, and immune context are simultaneously informative, and whose principles will transfer to the full spectrum of glandular neoplasia.


## G. Challenging and unconventional aspects

Three aspects of this project are genuinely challenging, and their combination distinguishes the proposal from work currently underway in the field.

The first is the dual competence the project demands — and that makes it feasible. Implementation requires a combination of expertise that is rarely concentrated in a single investigator: deep familiarity with the histological appearance of BE at the cellular and architectural level, together with the technical capability to design and train hierarchical graph models, spatial transcriptomic analysis pipelines, and self-supervised learning frameworks. My clinical training in anatomic pathology and established record in computational pathology, cancer genomics, immune microenvironment, and translational research (Ercan, 2023, PhD thesis, University of Basel) provide precisely this combination, demonstrated across projects spanning: spatially integrated molecular and immune ecology features that stratify Barrett's oesophagus cancer progression risk from H&E slides (Ercan et al., 2025, medRxiv); spatial immune microenvironment analysis with a computational model for solid tumour immunophenotyping (Ercan et al., 2024, Cancer Research); spatial genomic clonal analysis of a precursor lesion and matched malignancy in hepatocarcinogenesis (Ercan et al., 2022, Communications Medicine); weakly supervised learning for molecular predictions combined with spatial analysis (Ercan et al., 2024, PLMR); tumour stroma as a key element of the TME and its interactions with tumour and immune microenvironment (Coto-Llerena & Ercan et al., 2020, Frontiers in Oncology); and deep learning H&E morphology integrated with immune features for pathological assessment (Ercan et al., 2024, Virchows Archiv). The identification of an appropriate analysis strategy for BE tissue, the design of biologically informed graph edges, and the validation of attention-based model outputs against histological ground truth each require pathological expertise not substitutable by computational skill alone; the development of the graph architecture, the SSL training framework, and the Xenium analysis pipeline each require computational depth not substitutable by domain expertise alone.

The second challenging aspect is the interdisciplinary architecture of the work itself. The project requires expertise in at least four distinct domains simultaneously: the histological appearance of BE tissue at cell and crypt resolution; the design and training of hierarchical graph models with self-supervised objectives; the application of landscape ecology spatial statistics to biological tissue; and the analysis of single-cell spatial transcriptomic data in a multi-compartment precancer system. These disciplines rarely converge in a single research programme, and the specific combination — using ecological spatial statistics as explicit node-level features within a deep learning graph framework applied to a human precancer — has no prior precedent in computational pathology. The collaborative network assembled for this project reflects these requirements directly, spanning evolutionary biology and ecological spatial statistics (Maley, Brown), machine learning (Rapsomaniki), BE pathology (Odze, Stachler), oncology (Homicsko), and spatial transcriptomics and statistical genomics (Gottardo).

The third is the structural uniqueness of BE as a study system for precancer biology, and the resulting transferability of what is learned here. Barrett's oesophagus is followed endoscopically over years to decades without excision — a disease-management paradigm that produces the kind of longitudinal, multi-timepoint biopsy archive from the same patients that is structurally unavailable in most other epithelial precancer systems. Barrett's glands exhibit phenotypic and ecological complexity unique within the human gastrointestinal epithelium, sustaining diverse metaplastic cell populations under continuous selective pressure from reflux, inflammation, and treatment effects; this complexity makes BE a model system for studying the co-evolution of epithelium and microenvironment in ways that have not been computationally characterised. When this project successfully captures the ecology and evolution of BE, the methodological, morphological, and biological knowledge gained transfers directly to other precancer lesions where immune co-evolution and spatial tissue dynamics shape progression — from gastric intestinal metaplasia to inflammatory bowel disease-associated dysplasia to biliary precancer — providing a platform for interrogation of precancerous ecology across the spectrum of gastrointestinal neoplasia.
